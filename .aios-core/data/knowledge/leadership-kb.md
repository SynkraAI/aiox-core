## 1. Start With Why - Golden Circle

### 1.1 Simon Sinek's Golden Circle

```
The Golden Circle:

         ┌─────────────┐
         │             │
         │    WHY      │   Purpose, cause, belief
         │             │   "Why does our organization exist?"
         │  ┌───────┐  │
         │  │ HOW   │  │   Process, values, differentiators
         │  │       │  │   "How do we do what we do?"
         │  │┌─────┐│  │
         │  ││WHAT ││  │   Products, services, features
         │  ││     ││  │   "What do we do?"
         │  │└─────┘│  │
         │  └───────┘  │
         └─────────────┘

Most organizations communicate OUTSIDE-IN: What → How → Why
Great leaders communicate INSIDE-OUT: Why → How → What

"People don't buy WHAT you do; they buy WHY you do it."
```

#### The WHY Discovery Process

```
Step 1: Gather Stories
├── Ask: "What experience made you most proud?"
├── Ask: "When did you feel most alive at work?"
├── Ask: "What would you do even if you weren't paid?"
└── Look for themes across stories

Step 2: Find the Pattern
├── What emotion keeps coming up?
├── What impact keeps being mentioned?
├── What action keeps being described?
└── The pattern IS your WHY

Step 3: Articulate the WHY
Template: "To [CONTRIBUTION] so that [IMPACT]"

Examples:
├── Apple: "To challenge the status quo so that individuals can think differently"
├── AIOX: "To amplify human capability through AI so that teams can focus on what matters"
└── Test: Does this make you emotional? If not, dig deeper.

Step 4: Align HOW and WHAT
├── HOW = Your values and guiding principles
├── WHAT = Your products and services
├── Everything should trace back to WHY
└── If a project doesn't connect to WHY, question it
```

### 1.2 The Infinite Game (Sinek)

```
Finite Games vs Infinite Games:

│ Aspect        │ Finite Game         │ Infinite Game          │
├───────────────┼─────────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Players       │ Known               │ Known and unknown      │
│ Rules         │ Fixed               │ Changeable             │
│ Objective     │ Win                 │ Keep playing           │
│ Duration      │ Defined end         │ No end                 │
│ Examples      │ Football, election  │ Business, education    │
│ Mindset       │ Beat the opponent   │ Outlast the game       │

Five Practices of Infinite Leadership:
1. JUST CAUSE: An idealized future state worth pursuing
2. TRUSTING TEAMS: Safe environments where people can be vulnerable
3. WORTHY RIVALS: Others who reveal our weaknesses
4. EXISTENTIAL FLEXIBILITY: Capacity for dramatic strategic shift
5. COURAGE TO LEAD: Willingness to make unpopular long-term decisions

Just Cause Criteria:
├── For something (not against)
├── Inclusive (invites others to join)
├── Service-oriented (primarily benefits others)
├── Resilient (can endure change)
└── Idealistic (big, bold, unachievable but worth pursuing)
```

### 1.3 Leaders Eat Last

```
Core Concept: The Circle of Safety

Leaders create an environment where people feel safe.
When people feel safe INSIDE the organization,
they can focus their energy on threats OUTSIDE.

The Four Chemicals of Leadership:

SELFISH Chemicals (Individual Survival):
├── Endorphins: Mask pain, enable persistence
│   └── Leader application: Push through hard work together
└── Dopamine: Goal completion, achievement
    └── Leader application: Set clear, measurable goals

SELFLESS Chemicals (Social Bonding):
├── Serotonin: Pride, status, recognition
│   └── Leader application: Public recognition, celebrate achievements
└── Oxytocin: Trust, love, friendship
    └── Leader application: Invest time with team, show vulnerability

The Danger Chemical:
└── Cortisol: Stress, anxiety, self-preservation
    └── Leader's job: REDUCE cortisol by creating safety

"The true price of leadership is the willingness to place
the needs of others above your own."
```

## 2. Dare to Lead - Brene Brown

### 2.1 Vulnerability as Strength

```
"Vulnerability is not weakness. It's our greatest measure of courage."

Vulnerability in Leadership:
├── Admitting "I don't know"
├── Asking for help
├── Saying "I was wrong"
├── Having difficult conversations
├── Making decisions without perfect information
├── Giving honest feedback
├── Holding people accountable
└── Challenging the status quo

Vulnerability is NOT:
├── Oversharing personal drama
├── Using emotions to manipulate
├── Being a pushover
├── Sharing without boundaries
└── Trauma-dumping on your team

Armor vs Daring Leadership:
├── Armor: Perfectionism → Daring: Healthy striving
├── Armor: Foreboding joy → Daring: Practicing gratitude
├── Armor: Numbing → Daring: Setting boundaries
├── Armor: Being a knower → Daring: Being a learner
└── Armor: Cynicism → Daring: Modeling clarity and kindness
```

### 2.2 BRAVING Trust Framework

```
B - BOUNDARIES
  "I respect your boundaries and when not clear, I ask.
   I'm willing to say no."

R - RELIABILITY
  "You do what you say you'll do. I can count on you."

A - ACCOUNTABILITY
  "You own your mistakes, apologize, and make amends.
   I can do the same."

V - VAULT
  "You don't share information or experiences that are not yours to share.
   I need to know that my confidences are kept."

I - INTEGRITY
  "You choose courage over comfort. You practice your values
   rather than just professing them."

N - NON-JUDGMENT
  "I can ask for what I need and you can ask for what you need.
   We can talk about how we feel without judgment."

G - GENEROSITY
  "You extend the most generous interpretation possible to the
   intentions, words, and actions of others."

Application:
- Use BRAVING as a self-assessment for leaders
- Use BRAVING as a team conversation about trust
- When trust breaks, identify which element was violated
- Address the specific element, not "trust" in general
```

### 2.3 Rumble Conversations

```
A Rumble = A discussion where:
├── We stay curious and generous
├── We lean into vulnerability
├── We stay committed to truth
├── We're willing to be wrong
├── We hold space for complexity

Rumble Starters:
├── "The story I'm making up is..."
├── "I'm curious about..."
├── "Help me understand..."
├── "What does success look like here?"
├── "Walk me through your thinking."
├── "Tell me more."
├── "What am I missing?"
└── "I'm struggling with..."

Rumble Rules:
1. No one gets to check out (physically or emotionally)
2. No back-channeling (if it needs to be said, say it in the room)
3. Own your part
4. Listen to understand, not to respond
5. Practice the pause (don't react immediately)
6. Be willing to be wrong
7. Be kind AND honest (not one or the other)
```

### 2.4 Shame Resilience

```
Shame vs Guilt:
├── Shame: "I AM bad" (identity) → Destructive
├── Guilt: "I DID something bad" (behavior) → Can be constructive
└── Leaders should foster guilt (accountability) not shame

Shame Resilience Process:
1. Recognize shame (physical symptoms: flushing, chest tight, stomach drop)
2. Practice critical awareness (what triggered it?)
3. Reach out (connection is the antidote to shame)
4. Speak shame (naming it reduces its power)

Leader's Role:
├── Create shame-free environments
├── Separate behavior from identity in feedback
├── Normalize mistakes as learning
├── Model shame resilience yourself
└── Address shame-inducing behaviors in others
```

## 3. Organizational Psychology - Adam Grant

### 3.1 Give and Take

```
Three Types of People in Organizations:

GIVERS: Contribute more than they take
├── Share knowledge freely
├── Help without keeping score
├── Mentor generously
├── Risk: Being exploited (bottom of success ladder)
├── Reward: Creating goodwill and networks (also top of success ladder)
└── Key: Successful givers set boundaries

TAKERS: Extract more value than they contribute
├── Self-serving behavior
├── Credit-taking
├── Strategic generosity (only when it benefits them)
├── Short-term gain, long-term reputation damage
└── Easy to spot in networks (few reciprocal connections)

MATCHERS: Tit-for-tat, keeping score
├── Fair and balanced exchanges
├── "I'll help you if you help me"
├── Most people are matchers
└── Punish takers, reward givers

The Paradox:
Both the most AND least successful people are GIVERS.
Difference: Successful givers have BOUNDARIES.
They are "otherish" (care about others AND themselves),
not "selfless" (care about others at expense of self).

Leader Action:
├── Hire for giving behavior
├── Protect givers from exploitation
├── Identify and reform takers
├── Create systems that reward generosity
└── Model giving behavior yourself
```

### 3.2 Think Again

```
The Art of Rethinking:

Modes of Thinking:
├── PREACHER: When our sacred beliefs are in jeopardy
├── PROSECUTOR: When we try to prove someone else wrong
├── POLITICIAN: When we try to win approval
└── SCIENTIST: When we're searching for truth (USE THIS ONE)

Thinking Like a Scientist:
├── Form hypotheses, not beliefs
├── Run experiments, don't make assumptions
├── Seek disconfirming evidence actively
├── Update views based on new data
├── Celebrate being proven wrong (you learned!)
└── Ask "What would change my mind?"

Confident Humility:
"Having faith in your capability while appreciating that
you may not have the right solution."

├── NOT arrogance: "I'm always right"
├── NOT insecurity: "I'm never right"
├── YES: "I believe I can figure this out,
│         and I'm open to being wrong."

Leader Application:
├── Reward people who change their minds (not just those who're consistent)
├── Assign "challenge networks" (devil's advocates)
├── Create psychological safety for disagreement
├── Ask "What evidence would change our plan?"
├── Regularly schedule "assumption reviews"
└── Model saying "I was wrong about X, and here's what I learned"
```

### 3.3 Originals

```
How to Champion New Ideas:

1. GENERATING IDEAS
   ├── Volume predicts quality (more ideas = more great ideas)
   ├── Broad experience fuels creativity
   ├── Procrastination can aid creativity (incubation)
   └── Diverse teams generate more original ideas

2. RECOGNIZING GOOD IDEAS
   ├── Creators are terrible judges of their own ideas
   ├── Peers are better evaluators than managers
   ├── The test: Would you bet on this idea?
   └── Intuition is unreliable for novel ideas

3. VOICING IDEAS
   ├── Start with weaknesses (surprise and disarm critics)
   ├── Repeat exposure (familiarity breeds acceptance)
   ├── Present to middle management (not just top)
   ├── Frame as improvement, not revolution
   └── Find allies who share the vision

4. TIMING
   ├── First-mover advantage is overrated (47% failure rate)
   ├── Fast followers often win (8% failure rate)
   ├── Strategic patience: Wait for market readiness
   └── But don't wait too long (analysis paralysis)

For Leaders:
├── Create idea submission systems
├── Reward idea volume, not just idea quality
├── Assign innovation time (Google's 20%, 3M's 15%)
├── Protect originals from groupthink
└── Kill "that's how we've always done it"
```

## 4. Netflix Culture - Patty McCord

### 4.1 The Netflix Culture Code

```
Core Philosophy:
"We're a team, not a family."
Families have unconditional love.
Teams have the best players for each position.

Key Principles:

1. FREEDOM AND RESPONSIBILITY
   ├── Treat people like adults
   ├── Remove rules, add context
   ├── Trust people to make good decisions
   ├── More freedom = more responsibility
   └── If someone can't handle freedom, they don't belong

2. HIGH TALENT DENSITY
   ├── Hire only "stunning colleagues"
   ├── Adequate performers get generous severance
   ├── The "keeper test": Would you fight to keep this person?
   ├── If no → Give them a generous package and move on
   └── A great workplace is stunning colleagues, not perks

3. CONTEXT, NOT CONTROL
   ├── Give people context (strategy, metrics, goals)
   ├── Let them figure out the how
   ├── Align on what, not how
   ├── Trust > approval processes
   └── If you need to approve everything, you hired wrong

4. HIGHLY ALIGNED, LOOSELY COUPLED
   ├── Clear strategy and goals (alignment)
   ├── Teams execute independently (loose coupling)
   ├── Minimize cross-team dependencies
   ├── Trust other teams to deliver
   └── Coordinate at boundaries, not within teams

5. HONEST FEEDBACK CULTURE
   ├── Say what you think, kindly and directly
   ├── 360-degree feedback regularly
   ├── No "brilliant jerks" (bad behavior kills culture)
   ├── Feedback is a gift, not a punishment
   └── If you wouldn't say it to their face, don't say it
```

### 4.2 McCord's Management Principles

```
The Six Key Ideas:

1. Treat people like adults
   ├── No attendance tracking
   ├── No vacation day counting
   ├── Expense policy: "Act in Netflix's best interest"
   └── Trust first, verify if needed

2. Tell people the truth about their performance
   ├── Continuous feedback, not annual reviews
   ├── "Start, Stop, Continue" format
   ├── Be direct but kind
   └── No feedback sandwiches (they dilute the message)

3. Let managers manage
   ├── Stop managing every decision
   ├── Create leaders, not followers
   ├── Push decision-making down
   └── Manager's job: Set context, not approve actions

4. Practice radical honesty about the business
   ├── Share financial data with everyone
   ├── Share strategy openly
   ├── Share bad news quickly
   └── If you can't trust people with information, why do you trust them with customers?

5. Debate vigorously
   ├── Encourage disagreement
   ├── Data wins over hierarchy
   ├── "Sunlight is the best disinfectant"
   └── If no one disagrees, someone isn't thinking

6. Build the team you need for the FUTURE
   ├── Not the team you needed for the past
   ├── Skills needed change as company grows
   ├── It's okay if people "graduate" (outgrow/undergrow the role)
   └── Loyalty to the mission, not to individuals
```

### 4.3 Anti-Patterns McCord Warns Against

```
DON'T:
├── Implement processes to prevent the 3% who abuse freedom
│   └── Instead: Address the 3% directly
├── Use perks to compensate for bad management
│   └── Instead: Fix the management
├── Create policies to avoid difficult conversations
│   └── Instead: Have the conversations
├── Promote based on tenure instead of capability
│   └── Instead: Promote based on what's needed next
├── Build consensus culture (everyone agrees)
│   └── Instead: Build debate culture (best idea wins)
├── Hire for "culture fit" (code for sameness)
│   └── Instead: Hire for "culture add" (diversity)
└── Offer loyalty in exchange for performance
    └── Instead: Offer an amazing team and challenging work
```

## 5. Work Rules - Laszlo Bock

### 5.1 Google's People Operations Principles

```
The 10 Work Rules:

1. GIVE YOUR WORK MEANING
   ├── Connect individual work to company mission
   ├── Help people see the impact of their work
   ├── Make mission visible in daily operations
   └── "People want to feel that their work matters"

2. TRUST YOUR PEOPLE
   ├── Default to transparency
   ├── Give people choices in how they work
   ├── Give people voice in decisions that affect them
   └── Remove approval layers

3. HIRE ONLY PEOPLE WHO ARE BETTER THAN YOU
   ├── Never compromise on quality
   ├── Take your time (it's worth it)
   ├── Hire for general cognitive ability + learning ability
   └── "A bad hire costs you 100x more than a slow hire"

4. DON'T CONFUSE DEVELOPMENT WITH MANAGING PERFORMANCE
   ├── Separate performance conversations from development ones
   ├── Performance: How are you doing? (evaluation)
   ├── Development: How can you grow? (coaching)
   └── Mixing them makes both less effective

5. FOCUS ON THE TWO TAILS
   ├── Study your best performers (what makes them great?)
   ├── Help your lowest performers (can they be coached up?)
   ├── The middle manages itself with the right culture
   └── 5% on each end teaches you the most

6. BE FRUGAL AND GENEROUS
   ├── Spend on things that matter (tools, food, health)
   ├── Don't spend on things that don't (fancy offices for execs)
   ├── Most impactful perks cost nothing (flexibility, recognition)
   └── Pay unfairly (top performers deserve disproportionate rewards)

7. PAY UNFAIRLY
   ├── Top performers deliver 10x more value
   ├── Pay them accordingly (or they leave)
   ├── It's more fair to pay unequally based on impact
   ├── If everyone gets 3% raises, top performers are underpaid
   └── Make compensation proportional to contribution

8. NUDGE
   ├── Use behavioral science in people management
   ├── Default choices matter (opt-in vs opt-out)
   ├── Small changes in environment → big behavior changes
   └── Example: Smaller plates in cafeteria → healthier eating

9. MANAGE THE RISING EXPECTATIONS
   ├── Success creates expectations
   ├── Communicate openly about trade-offs
   ├── Not every perk can scale
   └── Focus on what matters most

10. ENJOY! AND THEN GO BACK TO #1.
    ├── Enjoy the journey of building great culture
    ├── Culture is never "done"
    ├── Iterate continuously
    └── Model the culture you want to see
```

### 5.2 Bock's Hiring Framework

```
The Four Attributes Google Hires For:

1. GENERAL COGNITIVE ABILITY
   ├── Not IQ, but learning ability
   ├── Can they solve novel problems?
   ├── Do they process information quickly?
   └── Can they connect disparate ideas?

2. ROLE-RELATED KNOWLEDGE
   ├── Can they do the job?
   ├── But be careful: Over-indexing on experience
   │   limits diverse thinking
   └── Balance experience with potential

3. LEADERSHIP
   ├── Not positional leadership
   ├── Emergent leadership: Do they step up when needed?
   ├── Do they step back when someone else should lead?
   └── Can they influence without authority?

4. GOOGLEYNESS (Culture Add)
   ├── Comfortable with ambiguity
   ├── Bias toward action
   ├── Collaborative
   ├── Intellectually humble
   └── Fun to work with

Interview Process:
├── Structured interviews (same questions for all candidates)
├── Behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time...")
├── Multiple interviewers (reduce individual bias)
├── Hire by committee (no single decision-maker)
├── Scoring rubric for each attribute
└── Rule of four: If four interviewers agree, that's enough
```

## 6. Radical Candor - Kim Scott

### 6.1 The Radical Candor Framework

```
                    CARE PERSONALLY
                         ↑
            ┌────────────┼────────────┐
            │            │            │
            │  Ruinous   │  Radical   │
            │  Empathy   │  Candor    │
            │            │            │
            │  "I don't  │  "I care   │
            │  want to   │  about you │
            │  hurt your │  AND your  │
 Don't ←────│  feelings" │  growth"   │────→ Do
Challenge   │            │            │   Challenge
Directly    │            │            │   Directly
            │  Manipu-   │  Obnoxious │
            │  lative    │  Aggression│
            │  Insinc.   │            │
            │            │            │
            │  "I don't  │  "I don't  │
            │  care and  │  care but  │
            │  won't     │  I'll tell │
            │  tell you" │  you"      │
            └────────────┼────────────┘
                         │
              DON'T CARE PERSONALLY

RADICAL CANDOR (top-right):
├── Care personally AND challenge directly
├── Praise specifically and sincerely
├── Criticize kindly and clearly
├── The goal is to help, not to harm
└── THIS IS THE GOAL

RUINOUS EMPATHY (top-left):
├── Care personally BUT don't challenge
├── "Nice" but not helpful
├── Withhold feedback to avoid discomfort
├── Most common management failure
└── Harms the person by denying growth

OBNOXIOUS AGGRESSION (bottom-right):
├── Challenge directly BUT don't care personally
├── "Brutal honesty" without empathy
├── Alienates and demotivates
└── Better than Ruinous Empathy (at least they know)

MANIPULATIVE INSINCERITY (bottom-left):
├── Neither care NOR challenge
├── Backstabbing, politics, passive-aggression
├── The worst quadrant
└── Toxic to culture
```

### 6.2 Giving Radical Candor

```
The COIN Framework for Feedback:

C - Context: When and where did the behavior happen?
O - Observation: What specific behavior did you observe?
I - Impact: What was the impact of that behavior?
N - Next steps: What should happen differently?

Example (Criticism):
"In yesterday's meeting (context), when you interrupted Maria
three times (observation), it prevented us from hearing her
analysis and made her disengage for the rest of the session
(impact). In future meetings, I'd like you to let people
finish their points before responding (next steps)."

Example (Praise):
"In the client presentation today (context), the way you
anticipated their concerns and addressed them proactively
(observation) meant we closed the deal on the spot instead
of another round of revisions (impact). I'd love to see
you mentor junior team members on this approach (next steps)."

Rules for Giving Feedback:
├── Give it immediately (within 48 hours)
├── Give criticism privately, praise publicly
├── Be specific (not "good job" or "bad job")
├── Focus on behavior, not personality
├── Make it a conversation, not a monologue
├── Ask for their perspective
├── Follow up on the outcome
└── FREQUENCY matters: Small, regular feedback > big annual review
```

### 6.3 Receiving Feedback

```
When Someone Gives You Feedback:

1. Listen without defending
   ├── Don't interrupt
   ├── Don't explain yourself
   ├── Don't argue
   └── Just listen

2. Clarify
   ├── "Can you give me an example?"
   ├── "What specifically would you suggest?"
   ├── "How did that impact you/the team?"
   └── Seek to understand, not to rebut

3. Thank them
   ├── It took courage to give you feedback
   ├── Even if you disagree, appreciate the intent
   └── "Thank you for telling me directly"

4. Reflect
   ├── Is there truth in this? (usually yes)
   ├── What part can I act on?
   ├── Is this a pattern? (ask others)
   └── What would change look like?

5. Act
   ├── Make visible changes
   ├── Tell them what you changed
   ├── Ask "Is this better?"
   └── Close the loop

Creating a Feedback Culture:
├── Leaders go first (ask for feedback before giving it)
├── "What could I do differently?"
├── Reward people who give you honest feedback
├── Make it safe to disagree with leadership
└── Share how you acted on feedback received
```

## 7. Hiring and Talent

### 7.1 Structured Hiring Process

```
Step 1: Define the Role
├── What does success look like in 6 months?
├── What are the 3-5 must-have skills?
├── What are nice-to-haves vs deal-breakers?
├── What's the growth trajectory for this role?
└── Write the scorecard (not just a job description)

Step 2: Source Candidates
├── Employee referrals (best source, but watch for homogeneity)
├── Targeted outreach (LinkedIn, communities)
├── Job boards (Indeed, specific niche boards)
├── Agencies (expensive but useful for hard-to-fill roles)
└── Internal pipeline (promote from within when possible)

Step 3: Screen (30 minutes)
├── Culture/values fit (brief check)
├── Role understanding (why this role?)
├── Motivation fit (why this company?)
├── Deal-breaker check (salary, location, timeline)
└── Decide: Move forward or stop

Step 4: Interview (3-4 rounds maximum)
├── Round 1: Technical/skill assessment (60 min)
├── Round 2: Behavioral interview (60 min)
├── Round 3: Team fit / working session (60 min)
├── Round 4: Final / leadership (30 min)
└── Each round has different interviewers and focus areas

Step 5: Evaluate
├── Structured scoring (rubric, not gut feeling)
├── Independent evaluations (before group discussion)
├── Group debrief (discuss, don't average)
├── Reference checks (ask behavioral questions)
└── Hire by committee when possible

Step 6: Offer and Close
├── Competitive compensation
├── Transparent about role expectations
├── Personalized closing (what motivates THIS person?)
├── Move fast (top candidates have options)
└── Onboarding plan ready before day 1
```

### 7.2 Behavioral Interview Questions

```
Framework: STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result)

Leadership:
├── "Tell me about a time you led a project through ambiguity."
├── "Describe a situation where you had to influence without authority."
├── "When did you make an unpopular decision? What happened?"
└── "Tell me about a time you had to give difficult feedback."

Problem Solving:
├── "Describe the hardest technical problem you've solved."
├── "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information."
├── "When did you disagree with your team's approach? What did you do?"
└── "Describe a time you identified a problem before anyone else."

Collaboration:
├── "Tell me about a conflict with a colleague. How did you resolve it?"
├── "Describe a time you helped someone on your team succeed."
├── "When did you have to work with someone you didn't get along with?"
└── "Tell me about your most successful team project and your role."

Growth Mindset:
├── "Tell me about your biggest professional failure. What did you learn?"
├── "Describe a time you received critical feedback. How did you respond?"
├── "When did you have to learn something completely new quickly?"
└── "Tell me about a time you changed your mind about something important."

Red Flags in Answers:
├── Always the hero (never credits others)
├── Blames others for failures
├── Can't give specific examples
├── No learning or growth in stories
├── Disrespects previous employers
└── Results are always vague ("it went well")
```

### 7.3 Onboarding Framework (90-Day Plan)

```
Week 1: ORIENT
├── Welcome and introduction to team
├── Tools and access setup (done BEFORE day 1)
├── Culture and values immersion
├── Buddy/mentor assigned
├── First quick win (small, achievable task)
└── Schedule 1:1 with manager and key stakeholders

Weeks 2-4: LEARN
├── Deep dive into product/service
├── Shadow experienced team members
├── Complete onboarding curriculum
├── Begin contributing to small projects
├── Weekly check-in with manager and buddy
└── Document questions and observations

Weeks 5-8: CONTRIBUTE
├── Take ownership of first real project
├── Begin participating in team rituals
├── Develop relationships across teams
├── Receive first structured feedback
├── Identify growth areas
└── Adjust 90-day goals if needed

Weeks 9-12: DELIVER
├── Complete first significant deliverable
├── Contribute independently to team goals
├── Demonstrate understanding of culture and process
├── 90-day review (mutual feedback)
├── Set goals for next quarter
└── Transition from "new hire" to "team member"

Success Metrics:
├── Time to first contribution (target: < 2 weeks)
├── Time to full productivity (target: < 3 months)
├── 30-day check-in satisfaction (target: > 8/10)
├── 90-day retention (target: > 95%)
└── Manager satisfaction with hire (target: > 4/5)
```

## 8. Team Dynamics

### 8.1 Psychological Safety (Amy Edmondson)

```
Psychological Safety = "A shared belief that the team is safe
for interpersonal risk-taking."

Behaviors in Psychologically Safe Teams:
├── People ask questions without fear of looking stupid
├── People admit mistakes without fear of punishment
├── People offer ideas without fear of ridicule
├── People challenge the status quo without retaliation
├── People give feedback directly and kindly
└── People ask for help when they need it

Google's Project Aristotle (Most Important Team Factors):
1. PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY (by far the most important)
2. Dependability (team members deliver on time)
3. Structure and clarity (clear roles, plans, goals)
4. Meaning (work is personally meaningful)
5. Impact (work matters to the organization)

Building Psychological Safety:
├── Leader goes first: Show vulnerability
├── Frame work as learning, not execution
├── Acknowledge your own mistakes publicly
├── Ask genuine questions (not gotchas)
├── Respond productively to bad news
├── Celebrate smart risks, even when they fail
└── Address interpersonal violations immediately
```

### 8.2 Team Development Stages (Tuckman)

```
1. FORMING
   ├── Polite, cautious, testing boundaries
   ├── Dependent on leader for direction
   ├── Unclear roles and processes
   ├── Leader action: Provide clarity, structure, and safety
   └── Duration: 1-2 weeks

2. STORMING
   ├── Conflicts emerge as people push back
   ├── Power struggles, disagreements on approach
   ├── Frustration with progress
   ├── Leader action: Facilitate conflict resolution,
   │   normalize disagreement, maintain direction
   └── Duration: 2-8 weeks (can be longer)

3. NORMING
   ├── Team develops shared norms and processes
   ├── Roles clarified, trust building
   ├── Collaboration improves
   ├── Leader action: Delegate more, empower team,
   │   reinforce positive norms
   └── Duration: 2-4 weeks

4. PERFORMING
   ├── High productivity and collaboration
   ├── Team is self-organizing
   ├── Conflicts resolved constructively
   ├── Leader action: Remove obstacles, provide strategic
   │   direction, celebrate wins
   └── Duration: Ongoing (maintenance required)

5. ADJOURNING
   ├── Project ends, team disbands
   ├── Celebrate achievements
   ├── Capture lessons learned
   ├── Leader action: Acknowledge contributions,
   │   facilitate transitions
   └── Duration: 1-2 weeks

Key Insight:
Any change (new member, new project, restructuring)
can send the team back to Forming or Storming.
```

### 8.3 High-Performing Team Characteristics

```
Characteristics of High-Performing Teams:

1. Clear Purpose
   ├── Everyone can articulate WHY the team exists
   ├── Goals are specific, measurable, and shared
   └── Individual goals align with team goals

2. Complementary Skills
   ├── Diverse backgrounds and perspectives
   ├── Mix of technical and interpersonal skills
   ├── T-shaped people (deep + broad)
   └── Skill gaps identified and addressed

3. Mutual Accountability
   ├── Team holds each other accountable (not just the leader)
   ├── Clear agreements on deliverables and quality
   ├── "We" language, not "I" or "they"
   └── Shared ownership of outcomes

4. Effective Communication
   ├── Information flows freely
   ├── Meetings are purposeful and efficient
   ├── Async communication for non-urgent items
   ├── Regular sync points for alignment
   └── Feedback is continuous and constructive

5. Trust and Safety
   ├── Psychological safety is high
   ├── Vulnerability is modeled by leaders
   ├── Mistakes are learning opportunities
   └── Conflict is constructive, not personal

Team Size:
├── Amazon's "Two Pizza Rule": If you can't feed the team
│   with two pizzas, it's too big
├── Optimal: 5-8 people (research supports this)
├── Above 8: Communication overhead grows exponentially
└── For larger groups: Split into sub-teams with clear boundaries
```

## 9. Performance Management

### 9.1 Modern Performance Management

```
Traditional (broken):
Annual review → Rating → Forced ranking → Compensation
Problems: Recency bias, political, demotivating, backward-looking

Modern (effective):
Continuous feedback → Regular 1:1s → Growth conversations → Fair compensation
Benefits: Timely, developmental, forward-looking, motivating

Performance = Ability × Motivation × Opportunity

If someone isn't performing, diagnose which factor:
├── Ability: Do they know how? → Training, coaching, pairing
├── Motivation: Do they want to? → Understand blockers, align goals
└── Opportunity: Can they? → Remove obstacles, provide resources
```

### 9.2 One-on-One Framework

```
Cadence: Weekly (30-60 min)
Owner: The REPORT owns the agenda, not the manager

Structure:
├── Check-in (5 min): How are you? (whole person, not just work)
├── Their agenda (15-25 min): What do they want to discuss?
├── Your agenda (10-15 min): What do you need to share/ask?
├── Development (5-10 min): Growth, career, learning
└── Actions (5 min): What are the clear next steps?

Manager's Job in 1:1s:
├── Listen more than talk (70/30 rule)
├── Ask questions, don't give answers
├── Provide context they don't have
├── Remove blockers they can't remove themselves
├── Coach for growth, not just output
└── Check for signs of burnout or disengagement

Questions to Ask:
├── "What's the most important thing we should talk about?"
├── "What's blocking you?"
├── "What would you do if you were me?"
├── "What have you learned this week?"
├── "How can I better support you?"
├── "What are you most excited about? Most worried about?"
└── "Is there anything you're not telling me?"

Red Flags (skip 1:1 at your peril):
├── Surprised by someone's resignation
├── Learning about problems from others
├── Team members going around you to your boss
├── Declining performance without clear cause
└── People not bringing up issues until they're critical
```

### 9.3 OKR Framework

```
OKR = Objectives and Key Results

Objective: Qualitative, inspiring, time-bound
├── What do we want to achieve?
├── Should be ambitious (stretch goals)
├── Should be memorable and motivating
└── Example: "Become the most loved product in our category"

Key Results: Quantitative, measurable, achievable
├── How will we know we achieved it?
├── 3-5 per objective
├── Should be measurable (number, %, date)
└── 70% achievement = good (if 100%, they weren't ambitious enough)

Example:
Objective: "Deliver a world-class onboarding experience"
├── KR1: Reduce time-to-value from 7 days to 1 day
├── KR2: Increase onboarding completion rate from 40% to 80%
├── KR3: Achieve NPS >50 from users in first 30 days
└── KR4: Reduce support tickets from new users by 60%

OKR Cadence:
├── Annual: Company-level objectives (big picture)
├── Quarterly: Team-level OKRs (aligned to company)
├── Weekly: Check progress, adjust tactics
├── Mid-quarter: Formal review, calibrate confidence
└── End-of-quarter: Score, reflect, plan next quarter

Common Mistakes:
├── Too many OKRs (max 3-5 objectives)
├── KRs that are tasks, not outcomes
├── No alignment between team and company OKRs
├── Setting easy OKRs to guarantee 100%
├── Tying OKRs directly to compensation (kills ambition)
└── Setting and forgetting (no weekly check-ins)
```

## 10. Decision Frameworks & Checklists

### 10.1 Hiring Decision Checklist

- [ ] Role scorecard clearly defined (outcomes, not tasks)
- [ ] Diverse candidate pipeline (>50% from underrepresented groups)
- [ ] Structured interview process (same questions for all)
- [ ] Multiple interviewers with different perspectives
- [ ] Scoring rubric used (not gut feeling)
- [ ] Independent evaluations before group discussion
- [ ] Reference checks completed (behavioral questions)
- [ ] "Keeper test" applied (would you fight to keep them?)
- [ ] Compensation is competitive and fair
- [ ] Onboarding plan ready before offer
- [ ] Bias check: Would you evaluate this candidate differently if they were a different gender/race/age?
- [ ] Culture ADD (not just culture fit)

### 10.2 Team Health Assessment

```
Rate each dimension 1-5:

□ PURPOSE: Does everyone know WHY we exist?
□ GOALS: Are goals clear, measurable, and shared?
□ ROLES: Does everyone know their responsibilities?
□ TRUST: Do people feel safe taking risks?
□ COMMUNICATION: Does information flow freely?
□ ACCOUNTABILITY: Do people hold each other accountable?
□ FEEDBACK: Is feedback regular, specific, and constructive?
□ DEVELOPMENT: Are people growing and learning?
□ INCLUSION: Does everyone feel they belong?
□ ENERGY: Are people motivated and engaged?

Score:
40-50: High-performing team
30-39: Good team, focus on weak areas
20-29: Struggling team, needs intervention
10-19: Dysfunctional, likely losing people

Action:
├── Share results transparently with team
├── Discuss the 2-3 lowest areas
├── Co-create action plan (team owns solutions)
├── Re-assess in 6 weeks
└── Celebrate improvements
```

### 10.3 Difficult Conversation Preparation

```
Before the Conversation:
- [ ] What is the specific issue? (observable behavior, not personality)
- [ ] What is the impact? (on team, project, business)
- [ ] What is my desired outcome?
- [ ] What might their perspective be? (assume positive intent)
- [ ] What facts do I have? (separate facts from stories)
- [ ] Am I in the right emotional state? (calm, caring, not angry)

During the Conversation:
- [ ] Start with care: "This matters to me because you matter to the team"
- [ ] State the observation: "I noticed that..."
- [ ] Share the impact: "The effect was..."
- [ ] Ask their perspective: "Help me understand your view"
- [ ] Listen actively (don't plan your response while they talk)
- [ ] Agree on next steps: "What can we do differently?"
- [ ] Offer support: "How can I help?"
- [ ] Set follow-up: "Let's check in on [date]"

After the Conversation:
- [ ] Document key points and agreements
- [ ] Follow through on your commitments
- [ ] Check in at agreed time
- [ ] Provide positive reinforcement when improvement happens
- [ ] Escalate if no improvement after 2-3 conversations
```

### 10.4 Leadership Self-Assessment

```
Monthly Reflection Questions:

Purpose & Direction:
├── Does my team know where we're going and why?
├── Have I communicated strategy clearly this month?
└── Are our priorities aligned with our goals?

People & Culture:
├── Have I given each person feedback this month?
├── Do I know what motivates each team member?
├── Have I addressed any toxic behavior immediately?
├── Am I creating psychological safety?
└── Have I recognized great work publicly?

Execution & Results:
├── Are we on track for our OKRs?
├── Have I removed blockers for my team?
├── Am I making decisions at the right speed?
└── Are meetings productive and necessary?

Growth & Development:
├── Am I investing in my team's growth?
├── Have I had career conversations this month?
├── Am I delegating enough?
├── What have I learned this month?
└── Am I seeking feedback on my leadership?

Self-Care:
├── Am I modeling sustainable work habits?
├── Am I taking care of my own wellbeing?
├── Do I have a peer support network?
└── Am I making time for strategic thinking?
```
