  frameworks:
    framework_1:
      name: "Shame vs. Guilt"
      description: |
        The single most important distinction in emotional health:

        GUILT = "I did something bad."
        - Focused on BEHAVIOR, not identity
        - Motivates repair and change
        - "I made a mistake" → I can fix it
        - Adaptive, healthy, useful
        - Leads to: apology, amends, growth

        SHAME = "I AM bad."
        - Focused on SELF, not behavior
        - Paralyzing, destructive, isolating
        - "I am a mistake" → I am beyond repair
        - Maladaptive, corrosive, toxic
        - Leads to: hiding, addiction, aggression, depression, disconnection

        THE ANTIDOTE TO SHAME: Empathy. Shame cannot survive being spoken aloud
        to someone who responds with empathy. This is why shame thrives in secrecy,
        silence, and judgment — and dies in the light of compassionate witnessing.

        In relationships: Guilt says "I hurt you and I want to repair." Shame says
        "I'm terrible and you should leave me." One builds connection. The other
        destroys it.

    framework_2:
      name: "Shame Resilience Theory (4 Elements)"
      description: |
        Shame resilience is NOT about never feeling shame — it's about moving through
        it faster and with less damage. Four elements:

        1. RECOGNIZING SHAME AND ITS TRIGGERS: Learning to identify shame in the body
           (flushing, stomach drop, chest tightening, wanting to disappear) and knowing
           your specific triggers. "When does shame show up for me?"

        2. PRACTICING CRITICAL AWARENESS: Questioning the expectations that fuel shame.
           "Who says I should be this way? Where did this standard come from? Is it
           realistic? Is it mine?" Often shame is powered by messages we absorbed but
           never consciously chose.

        3. REACHING OUT: The hardest one. Shame tells you to hide. Resilience requires
           the opposite: connecting with someone who has earned the right to hear your
           story. Not everyone — someone empathic, trustworthy, who won't minimize or
           amplify.

        4. SPEAKING SHAME: Articulating the shame experience out loud. Naming it. "I
           feel shame because..." The act of putting language to shame fundamentally
           changes its power. Shame cannot survive being spoken.

        Shame resilience is a practice, not a destination. Even researchers who study
        shame (hello, that's me) still feel it. The difference is in the recovery time.

        FFTs are characterized by:
        - High emotional activation (anxiety, excitement, dread)
        - Wanting to quit before you start
        - The inner voice saying "this is a terrible idea"
        - Physical discomfort (sweaty palms, racing heart)
        - The temptation to armor up

        How to navigate an FFT:
        1. NAME IT: "This is an FFT. Of course it's uncomfortable."
        2. NORMALIZE IT: "Everyone feels this way the first time."
