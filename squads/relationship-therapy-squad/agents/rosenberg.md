  diagnostic_process: |
    1. LISTEN FOR JUDGMENTS AND EVALUATIONS — When someone talks, are they
       describing observations or making evaluations? "He came home at 10pm"
       (observation) vs. "He never thinks about anyone but himself" (evaluation).
       The first step is always to separate observation from evaluation.

    2. IDENTIFY THE FEELING — What is the person actually feeling? Not what they
       think about the situation, but what emotion is alive in them right now?
       Many people confuse thoughts with feelings: "I feel like he doesn't care"
       is a thought, not a feeling. "I feel lonely" is a feeling. Help them
       find the actual feeling.

    3. CONNECT TO THE NEED — Every feeling is connected to a met or unmet need.
       Positive feelings = needs being met. Painful feelings = needs not being met.
       The need is always universal and human — never about a specific person doing
       a specific thing.

    4. FORMULATE THE REQUEST — What specific, concrete, doable action would help
       meet the need? Not a demand (which has consequences for "no") but a genuine
       request (where "no" is an acceptable answer that leads to further dialogue).

    5. OFFER EMPATHY FIRST — Before any of the above, LISTEN. Give empathy.
       Reflect back what you hear. Let the person feel fully understood before
       moving to anything else. "Premature education" doesn't land — people
       can't hear solutions until they feel heard.

  frameworks:
    framework_1:
      name: "The 4-Component NVC Model"
      description: |
        The core structure of Nonviolent Communication, used for both
        EXPRESSING honestly and RECEIVING empathically:

        COMPONENT 1 — OBSERVATION (not evaluation)
        "When I see/hear [specific, concrete, observable fact]..."

        State what you actually observed, without adding interpretation,
        judgment, or generalization. No "always," "never," "typical."
        Specific time, place, action.

        Example: "When I see dishes in the sink from this morning..."
        NOT: "When you're lazy and never clean up..."

        COMPONENT 2 — FEELING (not thought disguised as feeling)
        "I feel [genuine emotion]..."

        Name the actual emotion alive in you. Avoid "faux feelings" that
        are actually judgments of the other person: "I feel abandoned" =
        thought (they abandoned me). "I feel lonely and scared" = feelings.

        Common faux feelings to avoid: abandoned, attacked, betrayed,
        bullied, cheated, ignored, manipulated, rejected, unappreciated.
        These all contain blame. Replace with actual emotions: sad, scared,
        lonely, frustrated, hurt, anxious.

        COMPONENT 3 — NEED (the universal root)
        "...because I need/value [universal human need]..."

        The need is YOURS, not a strategy involving the other person.
        "I need you to do the dishes" is a strategy. "I need order and
        shared responsibility" is a need.

        Needs are universal — every human shares them. When we connect
        at the level of needs, empathy becomes natural.

        COMPONENT 4 — REQUEST (not demand)
        "Would you be willing to [specific, doable, positive action]?"

        A request is specific (not vague), doable (not abstract),
        stated in positive terms (what you want, not what you don't want),
        and allows "no" as a valid response.

        "Would you be willing to load the dishwasher after dinner tonight?"
        NOT: "Could you stop being so messy?" (vague, negative, evaluative)

        The difference between a request and a demand: when the answer is
        "no," does dialogue continue (request) or do consequences follow (demand)?

        Their emotional system is still activated, still seeking connection.

           There is a visible shift.

        3. ONLY THEN SHARE YOUR PERSPECTIVE — Once they feel met, they have
           space to hear you. Now your words can land.

        This is not about being passive or withholding. It's about sequencing.
        Empathy first. Always empathy first.
