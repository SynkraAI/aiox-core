  frameworks:
    framework_1:
      name: "The Polyvagal Ladder (3 States)"
      description: |
        The autonomic nervous system operates in three hierarchical states, like a
        ladder we move up and down:

        TOP — VENTRAL VAGAL (Safe & Connected):
        - Feeling: present, calm, curious, engaged, open, playful
        - Body: relaxed face, soft eyes, prosodic voice, open posture
        - Story the state generates: "The world is safe. I am connected. Things
          are manageable."
        - Capacity: full access to social engagement, creativity, problem-solving,
          intimacy, joy
        - This is where we can love, connect, and be truly present with another person

        MIDDLE — SYMPATHETIC (Fight or Flight):
        - Feeling: anxious, activated, angry, hypervigilant, restless, irritable
        - Body: tense muscles, racing heart, shallow breathing, clenched jaw
        - Story the state generates: "I am in danger. I must fight or flee. I
          cannot trust."
        - Capacity: mobilized for action but CANNOT generate feelings of safety.
          Cannot access social engagement system. Looks for threats everywhere.
        - CRITICAL: You cannot think your way out of this state. You must regulate
          your way out. The body must shift before the mind can.

        BOTTOM — DORSAL VAGAL (Shutdown):
        - Feeling: collapsed, disconnected, numb, dissociated, hopeless, frozen
        - Body: flat affect, low energy, monotone voice, collapsed posture
        - Story the state generates: "There is no hope. Nothing matters. I am alone."
        - Capacity: minimal. Conservation mode. The system has decided the threat is
          too overwhelming to fight or flee from — so it shuts down.
        - This is not laziness or depression by choice — it is a biological protection.

        We move up and down this ladder all day. Health is not staying at the top —
        it is the flexibility to move and the ability to RETURN to ventral.

        When you are in VENTRAL: "My partner is just having a bad day. We'll work it out."

        IMPLICATION: You cannot think your way into a new state. You must REGULATE
        your way there — and the new state will generate a new story automatically.

        This is why "just think positive" doesn't work. You cannot install a ventral
        story on a sympathetic operating system. Change the state first. The story
        follows.

    framework_3:

      description: |
        TRIGGERS: Cues of danger that pull you DOWN the polyvagal ladder.
        - A certain tone of voice, a facial expression, a sound, a memory, a smell
        - Activate sympathetic (fight/flight) or dorsal (shutdown)
        - Feel automatic and overwhelming
        - Are specific to YOUR nervous system and YOUR history

        GLIMMERS: Micro-moments of ventral vagal activation — the OPPOSITE of triggers.
        - A shaft of sunlight through a window
        - The warmth of a pet against your body
        - A specific song
        - Eye contact with someone safe
        - The smell of coffee in the morning
        - A gentle breeze on skin
        - The sound of a particular person's laugh

        KEY INSIGHT: Glimmers are TRAINABLE. The more you notice them, the more your
        nervous system learns to find them. This is not "positive thinking" — it is
        neuroplasticity. You are literally training your neuroception to detect safety.

        PRACTICE: Three glimmers a day. Just notice them. Name them. "That was a
        glimmer." Over time, your nervous system builds a larger library of safety cues.

    framework_4:

      description: |
        Human nervous systems are designed to regulate THROUGH each other. This is not
        weakness or dependency — it is species design. We are biologically built for
        co-regulation.

        How it works: A regulated nervous system (ventral) sends cues of safety to a
        dysregulated nervous system — through tone of voice, facial expression, body
        language, presence, and rhythmic interaction. The dysregulated system "borrows"
        regulation from the regulated one.

        This is why:
        - A calm parent soothes a crying baby (not through words — through state)
        - A therapist's regulated presence IS the intervention
        - Being near certain people makes you feel instantly calmer
        - Phone calls with certain friends regulate you even when they say nothing profound

        In relationships: Co-regulation is the invisible foundation. When partners can
        regulate each other, conflict is manageable. When co-regulation breaks down,
        everything feels threatening.

        The capacity for self-regulation GROWS FROM co-regulation. We don't learn to
        calm ourselves alone — we learn it first through being calmed by another, then
        internalize that capacity.

        4. RE-STORY: From the new, regulated state, construct a new narrative. "From
           ventral, what do I see now? What story is available to me from this state
           that wasn't available from the previous one?"

        ORDER MATTERS: You cannot re-story from a dysregulated state. Regulate first.
        Story follows state.

        - Temperature change: splash cold water on face, hold ice
        - Orienting: slowly look around the room, name 5 things you see

        - Breathing: extend the exhale (longer exhale activates ventral vagal)
        - Social engagement: eye contact with safe person, hearing soothing voice
        - Bilateral stimulation: walking, tapping alternating sides
        - Co-regulation: being near a regulated nervous system
        - The goal is safe landing — softening the mobilization into connection

        GENTLENESS IS REQUIRED at every transition. The nervous system needs
        permission to move. Forcing it will trigger a protective response.

    framework_7:

        Neuroception is the body's unconscious, continuous scanning for cues of
        safety and danger. It operates BEFORE conscious perception — below awareness.

        Neuroception precedes perception.

        This means:
        - You can feel scared without knowing why
        - You can feel unsafe in a "safe" environment
        - You can feel drawn to someone without conscious reason
        - Your body can react before your mind catches up

        Neuroception reads:
        - Facial expressions (especially eyes and mouth)
        - Tone of voice (prosody — melodic vs. flat)
        - Body language and postural cues
        - Environmental cues (lighting, space, noise)
        - Internal body cues (heart rate, gut feelings)

        "Faulty neuroception" is when the system detects danger where there is none
        (common in trauma survivors) or misses danger where it exists. It is not the
        person being "dramatic" or "naive" — it is the body's calibration.

        Understanding neuroception explains why telling a traumatized person "you're
        safe now" doesn't work — their neuroception is still detecting threat,
        regardless of what the conscious mind knows.
